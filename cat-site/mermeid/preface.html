Preface

Francesco Pollini (1762-1846) was an amateur composer, tenor, pianist, and piano teacher who shaped his education in Vienna - where he met Wolfgang Amadé Mozart - during the 1780s. Born in Ljubljana, after his first instruction in the capital of the Empire, he moved to Milan (around 1792) where he settled definitively. There he was active as a teacher and a composer, producing an influential piano method and a consistent number of piano works, vocal chamber music and also operas.
The majority of Pollini compositions were published during his Milanese years under his authority and are held at the Milan Conservatory Library (I-Mc). Here and in other libraries (like in the Giulio Einaudi Library of Correggio, I-CORc or in Giuseppe Greggiati Library of Ostiglia, I-OS) are also preserved many manuscript sources. An overview of Pollini’s output entails an assessment of his achievements and permits to locate his personality in the context of the professional strategies of a musician in his time: as a pedagogue, composer and performer – though he was, because of his family’s activities, not dependent on an income from the musical world. Assessing Pollini’s compositional output, life strategies and self-understanding as a musician also permits to investigate concepts of nationality in the instrumental music of the period. The early Italian piano school, from the late 18th century and early 19th century, had a direct filiation from the classical Viennese piano school: in this context, Pollini represents the most ideal bridge between these two traditions.
